Premortem Report

Shark Branding Solutions Website

7 failure agents · May 17, 2026

"It is 6 months from now. The website has failed to generate meaningful paying clients. We are looking back to understand why."

Synthesis

Most Likely Failure

The Audit Escape Valve + Abstract Pain

People request the audit (feels like taking action), get the report, never feel urgency because they've never experienced a missing call, and ghost. This is baked into the funnel structure — the audit is both the entry point and the exit. Without a hard next step forcing the conversation, it ends itself.

Most Dangerous Failure

Thin Social Proof Kills Referrals

One anonymous case study means prospects can't verify the work and won't refer. Without referrals, a local service business stalls. This compounds monthly: no named clients → no referrals → no new case studies → no social proof. It's the failure mode that locks the whole flywheel.

The Hidden Assumption

Awareness is the barrier. It isn't.

The website is built as if the biggest barrier to conversion is that prospects don't understand AI visibility — so if they understand it, they'll pay to fix it. But every failure mode traces back to trust, not awareness. Trust in the company. Trust in the outcome. Trust in the commitment structure. The website explains well. It doesn't prove well.

The Revised Plan — Concrete Changes

  • 1Kill the word "Agreement." Rename to "12-Month Program" and "3-Month Starter" — same terms, zero contract anxiety trigger. Test this week before the chamber push.
  • 2Get 3 named clients with real quotes before relying on the website for cold leads. Offer the first 3 months at cost to a local HVAC, dental, or contractor client in exchange for a named testimonial and a photo.
  • 3Build a 5-email follow-up sequence in Vendasta that fires after every audit — Day 1, 3, 7, 14, 30. Each email has one CTA: book a 20-minute call. Without this, the audit is an exit, not an entry.
  • 4Make the pain visible at chamber events. Open ChatGPT live, search "best HVAC company in Wesley Chapel," show the room whose names come up. That 60-second demo does more than the entire website.
  • 5Stop relying on chamber email clicks. Ask for 5–10 minutes at the next in-person meeting to do the live demo. The people who open chamber newsletters are not always the buyers.

Pre-Launch Checklist — Before the Chamber Newsletter Goes Out

☐ Set up automated Vendasta follow-up sequence (5 emails) for every audit lead
☐ Get at least 2 testimonials with real names and business names on the site
☐ Change "12-Month Agreement" → "12-Month Program" on chamber page and plans page
☐ Add a live demo step to discovery call script (open ChatGPT, search their category + city)
☐ Book 5 minutes at next chamber in-person meeting for the live ChatGPT demo

7 Failure Analyses

Each agent investigated one failure mode independently.

01 Price Without Proof of Concept
Failure Story

A Wesley Chapel plumber runs the free audit, then sees $397/month × 12 = $4,764. He has no mental model for "AI visibility" pricing. Is this cheap? Expensive? He closes the tab. Discovery calls that do happen stall at "what exactly am I getting?" — requiring three leaps of faith (AI search is real, it affects his business, this service moves the needle) before justifying the dollar amount. The anonymous HVAC case study doesn't connect to revenue and can't be verified. The price stays abstract because the outcome stays abstract.

Underlying Assumption
Showing the gap (the audit) is sufficient to justify the price, without proving revenue-connected outcomes.
Early Warning Signs
Booking-to-close rate on discovery calls sits below 15%
Prospects repeatedly ask "can I start month-to-month?" before going quiet
02 Free Audit as Escape Valve
Failure Story

Month 1: 11 audit requests. Celebration. By month 5: 47 requests, 2 paid clients — both referrals who would have converted anyway. Seven of the first eleven never replied to the report delivery email. Three said "interesting" and went cold. One got sticker shock. There was no automated follow-up sequence. Follow-up was a manual notebook task. Miche kept optimizing the website because it felt like the problem. The audit created a sense of completion without urgency — prospects felt they had "done something" and moved on.

Underlying Assumption
Delivering a valuable free report creates a sense of obligation to continue the conversation.
Early Warning Signs
Audit completion rate high but reply rate to delivered report below 30%
Discovery calls booked ÷ audits sent is under 15% by week six
03 Existing Vendor Loyalty
Failure Story

Chamber members who were genuinely interested mentioned the service to their current marketing person as a test. The marketing person said "yeah, we've been looking at that for you." That was enough. Owner felt relieved. No awkward conversation. No change. Only owners without an existing vendor converted. Shark Branding Solutions effectively educated the incumbent agencies, not the market. Replacing a nephew who "does the website" isn't a vendor decision — it's a family conversation nobody wanted to have over something as abstract as AI rankings they couldn't see.

Underlying Assumption
A compelling enough offer overrides the social and emotional inertia of existing vendor relationships.
Early Warning Signs
Calls end with "I need to run this by my current person" rather than a direct objection
Booked audits result in no-shows or last-minute reschedules above 30%
04 Thin Social Proof
Failure Story

Prospects landed, read the pitch, felt a flicker of interest, then looked for the one thing every buyer looks for before spending $400–$2,000/month: confirmation that someone else already did this and it worked. One anonymous case study with no name, no photo, no screenshot, no verifiable number. They Googled "Shark Branding Solutions reviews" and found nothing. The absence confirmed the fear: too new, too unproven. The anonymous HVAC case study had never once tipped a prospect into action — it couldn't answer the only question that mattered: can I talk to one of your current clients?

Underlying Assumption
A compelling result — even unattributed — is persuasive enough to carry the weight of an entirely unknown brand asking for recurring monthly spend.
Early Warning Signs
Visitors reach the case study section but drop off without submitting the form
Discovery calls stall at "I'd love to talk to one of your current clients"
05 Abstract Pain
Failure Story

The plumber had three leaky pipe calls and a parts invoice to dispute. He didn't search ChatGPT for himself. The dentist read the full pitch, nodded, forwarded it to her office manager with "look into this." The office manager had 17 other things open. The tab died. No one counted the patients who asked an AI first and booked somewhere else. Invisible losses don't trigger purchasing decisions. Six months in: 12 follow-up emails sent, 11 responded with "we're focused on other things right now." The website had great messaging for people who already understood AI visibility. Those people don't exist in Wesley Chapel yet.

Underlying Assumption
Explaining a problem clearly is the same as making someone feel it.
Early Warning Signs
Visitors spend time on page but leave without contacting — engagement without action
Sales conversations end with "I'll think about it" rather than objections about price or timeline
06 Wrong People Click
Failure Story

The chamber newsletter goes out Tuesday. Three people click within an hour: a marketing coordinator who bookmarks it to share with her boss (never does), a real estate agent who knows Miche but doesn't see the need, and a fellow service provider curious about the offer structure. The actual owners — the HVAC company owner, the orthodontist with three locations, the restaurateur — never open the email. They get 200 emails a week. Chamber newsletters are background noise. 47 link clicks, zero signed agreements. Several great conversations with chamber members who "loved the idea" — but those people were never going to buy.

Underlying Assumption
Chamber email engagement is a proxy for buying intent and decision-making authority.
Early Warning Signs
Clicks with near-zero form submissions in the first two weeks post-send
People who reach out are employees or fellow service providers, not business owners with staff
07 Contract Aversion
Failure Story

A dentist lands on the page after a Google search. The copy is good. He nods along, thinks "this is exactly what I've been missing." Scrolls to pricing. Eyes catch "12-Month Agreement." Stomach drops. 18 months ago he paid $1,200/mo to an SEO agency for a year, rankings never moved, ate the cancellation clause. He closes the tab. The 3-month option doesn't save it — it's $100 more per month and still says "Agreement." Reading as a penalty for not trusting a stranger. To a small business owner, "agreement" isn't neutral. It's the thing they signed before they got burned.

Underlying Assumption
A motivated buyer will push through contract anxiety because the service concept is compelling enough to override it.
Early Warning Signs
High scroll depth on pricing section + near-zero form submissions
"Let me think about it" immediately after hearing terms, with no follow-up questions about the service
